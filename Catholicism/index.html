Catholicism

The Catholic Church is the largest Christian denomination, with over 1.3 billion members worldwide. It is led by the Pope, who is considered the successor to Saint Peter, one of Jesus Christ’s apostles. The Church has a rich history, spanning over 2,000 years, and is known for its doctrines, sacraments, and global presence.

Some key beliefs in the Catholic Church include:

  • The Trinity:
    One God in three persons: the Father, the Son (Jesus Christ), and the Holy Spirit.

  • Jesus Christ:
    Jesus is the Son of God, born of the Virgin Mary, who became man, died for humanity’s sins, and rose from the dead.

  • The Bible:
    The Catholic Bible includes the Old and New Testaments, with 73 books in total, including seven deuterocanonical books not found in Protestant Bibles.

  • The Seven Sacraments:
    • Baptism
    • Eucharist (Holy Communion)
    • Confirmation
    • Reconciliation (Confession)
    • Anointing of the Sick
    • Matrimony (Marriage)
    • Holy Orders (ordination of priests)

  • The Virgin Mary and the Saints:
    The honour of Mary, the mother of Jesus, and the saints, who are viewed as holy individuals worthy of veneration. They are not worshipped but seen as intercessors who pray to God on behalf of believers.

  • Papal Authority:
    The Pope, as the Bishop of Rome, is considered the supreme authority in matters of faith and morals. His teachings are regarded as infallible under specific conditions (papal infallibility).

  • The Church’s Teachings:
    Based on a combination of Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition, the Church develops doctrines that guide its faithful.
